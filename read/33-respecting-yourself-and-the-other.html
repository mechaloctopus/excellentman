Chapter 33Respecting Yourself and the Other

5 min read

ITwo Sovereigns, or Nothing

Every failed courtship dynamic — the doormat, the player, the auditioner, the collector — reduces to the same broken geometry: one party positioned above, one below. The suitor groveling for selection has crowned her his judge; the player running inventory has crowned himself hers. Both geometries produce misery on schedule, because both are lies about what is actually standing in the room: two complete human beings, each sovereign (Book IX will finish that word, but its courtship meaning is available now) — neither auditioning, neither appraising; two whole persons discovering whether their lives are better woven together. Hold that image, because it is the corrective to every distortion this chapter treats. Not a buyer and a seller. Not a judge and an applicant. Two sovereigns, meeting — or nothing.

IITo Thine Own Self: The Standard

Polonius's line, so worn it reads as wallpaper, is load-bearing here: a man cannot bring truth to another until he has stopped lying to himself — and courtship's characteristic self-lie is the erasure of the standard. Watch the pattern: a man meets someone compelling and begins, immediately and invisibly, editing himself — his opinions softened toward hers, his enthusiasms shelved, his schedule liquefied, his deal-breakers renamed "things to work on." He calls this flexibility. It is auditioning — the systematic replacement of himself with a composite sketch of what he guesses she wants — and it fails on every level at once: strategically (she was drawn, if she was drawn, to the man he just discontinued), structurally (a bond formed with the sketch must either collapse when the real man resurfaces or condemn him to playing the sketch forever), and spiritually (Chapter 7: the interior return on self-erasure is instant — a man who abandons himself for approval respects himself less by nightfall, and brings that diminished man to the next date).

So the standard, held: know what you offer and what you require, and let both be visible. What you offer — honestly inventoried, because self-respect without self-honesty is just vanity (the man demanding a partner of depth and discipline while offering neither has confused standards with entitlement; Chapter 7's mirror comes first, always). What you require — the genuine architecture of your life: the values not for sale, the direction not negotiable, the treatment beneath which you will not live. Requirements are not arrogance; stated without cruelty, they are information the other sovereign is owed — and here is the reframe that dissolves the fear of holding them: a deal-breaker honored early is a mercy to both people. The man who suppresses his requirement to keep a courtship alive is not being generous; he is billing her, silently, for a future invoice she never saw. Standards, like the clean no they are made of (Chapter 12), are a form of honesty.

And underneath the standard, the disposition that makes it livable rather than rigid: wholeness before partnership. The man who arrives at courtship as a half seeking completion has assigned the other person an impossible job — be my meaning, my regulation, my missing structure — and no human being holds that posting long without suffocating or fleeing. The excellent man arrives already whole: a life with weight-bearing walls of its own — the work, the practices, the friendships, the daily architecture of Books I through IV — into which a partner is invited, not conscripted as scaffolding. This is not self-sufficiency as armor (the hard man's version — needing no one as a defense against everyone). It is the difference between a man who wants her and a man who needs saving, and every woman alive can tell them apart by the second evening.

IIIAnd the Other Sovereign

Now the mirror image, because the geometry breaks in both directions. Respecting her sovereignty means, first, the retirement of the appraisal stance — the evaluative squint that runs courtship as a scoring exercise, itemizing her against a checklist while performing warmth. People feel appraisal through any charm (it is the invoice again, Chapter 3, drawn on a different account), and its opposite is not standardlessness but regard: attending to the actual person — her aims, her tastes, her contradictions, her Tuesday — as a full subject in her own story rather than a candidate in yours. It means honoring her requirements as you hold your own: her deal-breakers received as information, not as negotiations to win; her pace as sovereign as your intent (Chapter 32's law, restated at the level of principle); her no — to anything, at any stage — as final on delivery, appeal denied, warmth retained. It means never keeping score — the ledger of who texted last, who conceded last, who is currently "winning," which is the buyer-seller geometry smuggled back in decimal form; between sovereigns there is no score, only the running question are we both more alive in this? And it means the quiet discipline the old books called never speaking ill of the beloved — to friends, to family, to the phone: the man narrating his courtship's private frictions to an audience has made her a character in his content, and sovereignty extends to her dignity in rooms she isn't in (Chapter 11's absent-person rule, at maximum stakes).

Held together — the standard and the regard, the requirements and the reverence — the two disciplines produce courtship's rarest and most attractive quality, for which the manual has been building vocabulary since Chapter 4: secure warmth. Open-handed and unhurried; fully interested and fully intact; capable of pursuing without chasing and of losing without collapsing — because the man's center was never placed in the outcome. That man can afford total honesty, first-date directness, gracious noes given and received, standards stated without armor — all the behaviors this Book prescribes — for the simple structural reason that nothing he cannot survive losing is on the table. Two sovereigns, or nothing, turns out to be not just the ethical geometry but the winning one. It always was.

IVThe Practice

Practice

This week: the two inventories. On paper, in the audit: the offer — what a partner actually receives in you, listed honestly, gaps included (the gaps become Book-assignments, not shame); and the requirement — the three to five genuine non-negotiables of your architecture, distinguished ruthlessly from preferences wearing armor. Then the geometry check on anything live: where in my current courtship — or my pattern across the last several — have I auditioned (self edited for approval), appraised (her itemized against checklist), or scored (the ledger of concessions)? Pick the worst distortion and correct it this week in one concrete act: the opinion un-softened, the requirement stated kindly, the scorekeeping app in your head deleted. Two sovereigns. Begin conducting yourself as one, and watch what it invites.

The Line

Neither auditioning nor appraising — two whole lives, honestly offered.* --- ---
