Book VI · The World

Chapter 37The Traveler

6 min read

IThe Guest of Nations

Book IV trained the guest at a table. Book VI opens by scaling him to his largest deployment: the guest of an entire country. Because that is what a traveler is — the reframe from which every rule in this chapter descends, and the one the worst travelers have precisely inverted. The tourist-as-customer moves through foreign places as through a purchased experience: the country a venue, the locals staff, the differences defects to be complained about in his own language at increasing volume. The excellent man travels under Chapter 24's ancient contract instead — xenia, now with a passport: he has been admitted into someone else's home at national scale, and he owes what guests owe — conduct, gratitude, curiosity, and measure. Everything else is detail.

And the detail begins before departure, with the guest's basic diligence: the homework. Ten phrases of the local language, learned in the departure lounge if nowhere else — hello, please, thank you, excuse me, goodbye, yes, no, "do you speak English?", "the bill, please," and one honest compliment. The fluency is irrelevant; the attempt is everything — it is the traveler's version of the host gift, a small physical proof that you did not expect a nation to rearrange itself around your mother tongue, and it transforms receptions everywhere on earth (the waiter who endures English demanded will beam at English requested in broken Portuguese). Alongside the phrases, the customs file: the tipping norms (below), the dress expectations (churches, mosques, temples, and conservative streets have codes, and Chapter 22's principle — respect of the occasion — does not expire at borders; the shoulders covered and the shoes removed where asked are not impositions on your style but the entry fee of someone else's sacred), the greeting forms (the bow's depth, the cheek-kiss count, the handshake's local grammar), the table's choreography (Chapter 26 packed its section for exactly this trip), and the handful of local don'ts every guidebook lists in its first chapter and every oblivious traveler demonstrates by lunchtime.

IIThe Rules of the Road

In transit. The airport and airplane are the commons (Chapter 23) at maximum compression — strangers in stress, sharing armrests — and the disciplines are the directive miniaturized: the security line prepared for before reaching the bin (the man dismantling his wardrobe at the scanner is taxing a hundred people behind him); the boarding process met with calm rather than the gate-crowding scrum; the middle seat conceded both armrests (the one law of aviation everyone knows and the selfish test everyone grades); the recline performed with a glance behind and never at meal service; the shoes on, the socks private, the seat-back not used as a climbing wall on exit; children met with charity (every parent aloft is having a harder flight than you) and the crew met with names read off the badge and please-and-thank-you throughout — they are the hosts of the tube, and Chapter 24's guest knows it. Delays and cancellations, meanwhile, are the traveler's composure exam (Chapter 6, scheduled by the airline): the man at the rebooking desk who is calm, brief, and courteous to the agent — who did not cause the thunderstorm — is not only behaving excellently; he is, as every road warrior knows, the man who gets rebooked best, because the Law of Return operates with unusual speed at a service counter.

In residence. Hotels and rentals are households, staffed by people whose week you affect: the room kept decent daily (the housekeeper is not an excuse for regression to squalor — Chapter 24's overnight guest did not check out of his standards), the towels and do-not-disturb signals used as the systems they are, the noise governed by the hour and the walls, the staff greeted as humans rather than furniture — the doorman's name learned by day two is the oldest trick of the great travelers, because it is not a trick. And tipping, the traveler's perennial anxiety, resolved by one principle plus homework: the principle — tipping is part of the local wage architecture, not a performance of your generosity — and the homework: learn the local norm and follow it, because both errors insult (the under-tip in Manhattan stiffs a worker whose wage assumes it; the ostentatious over-tip in Tokyo, where service is priced into pride, can genuinely offend). Where the norm is service-included, believe it; where it is 15–20 percent, budget it as part of the price; where it is the porter's coin and the guide's note, carry small bills always — the excellent traveler's pocket is never caught unable to thank someone.

Among the people. The photographs taken of people are taken with permission — a raised camera and a questioning smile is a universal sentence — and never of the mourning, the praying, or the poor as spectacle; the haggling, where haggling is the culture, played as the social game it locally is (cheerfully, with the understanding that the last dollar you are grinding for lunch money matters differently to the vendor than to you); the voice kept at local volume (the traveling pack audible across three train cars has broadcast its passport before anyone sees it); the comparisons kept interior (every "well, back home we—" is the customer talking; the guest asks how it's done here, and why, and listens — Twain's fatality to prejudice operates precisely through that question, asked honestly, at a hundred tables); and the mistakes — you will make them; the bow botched, the wrong hand extended, the sacred stepped on in ignorance — repaired the only way they ever are: the immediate, evident, humble apology, which the whole world's cultures recognize and nearly always forgive, because goodwill is the actual universal language and Chapter 26 already taught you: the table forgives the wrong fork instantly; it never forgets a cold heart.

And leave it better. The trail packed out, the reef unstepped-on, the review written for the small family place that fed you like kin, the guide thanked by name to his employer, the promise to send the photo kept (Chapter 12 travels too). The excellent man's passport stamps are not trophies of consumption; they are a ledger of households briefly joined — and in every one of them, someone should be glad he came.

IIIThe Practice

This trip (and there is one coming; plan it if not): travel as the guest. Before departure: the ten phrases learned aloud, the customs file built (one hour, once), the tipping norms noted, small bills arranged. In transit: the commons disciplines run in full, and one service worker per leg treated with deliberate, named courtesy. In residence: the staff greeted as households deserve; the room kept to your standards, not the mercy of housekeeping. Among the people: one meal ordered entirely in the attempted local tongue, one "how is it done here?" conversation pursued with genuine curiosity to at least the second question (Chapter 10), zero back-home comparisons spoken aloud for the duration. And on return, the audit: what did the guest learn that the customer never would have been told?

The Line

A guest of the nation — learn ten words, follow the local lead, leave it better.* --- ---
