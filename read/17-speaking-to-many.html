Chapter 17Speaking to Many

7 min read

IThe Standing Man

At some hour you do not choose, it will be you: the glass tapped, the room turning, forty faces waiting — the wedding, the retirement, the funeral, the milestone birthday of someone you love. In that moment every man in the room is having one of two experiences: the common one, a spike of animal dread and gratitude that it isn't him — or the trained one, a quiet settling into a craft he has prepared, because he knew this hour was on the calendar of every full life and treated it accordingly.

This chapter is that preparation. And begin by shrinking the monster to its true size: the fear of speaking to many — surveys perennially rank it above the fear of death, which as the old joke observes means the average man at a funeral would rather be in the casket than doing the eulogy — is a misfiring of ancient equipment. The animal in you reads all those eyes as a predator situation. It is not. It is the opposite: a room of people actively wishing you well, hoping you succeed, because your success is their good evening. No audience at a toast wants blood; they want to feel something together, and you are merely the instrument they have agreed to feel it through. Reframe the job accordingly — you are not performing for the room; you are hosting a feeling on its behalf — and half the dread evaporates before technique even arrives. The rest yields to Chapter 6: the long exhale in the seconds before rising, the full height, the deliberate first sentence at three-quarter speed. The composed body persuades its owner along with the audience.

IIThe Toast

The toast is the form you will use most, so it gets the full craft. The architecture is three movements — hook, heart, lift — inside one iron constraint: sixty to ninety seconds. (The constraint is load-bearing. Every toast disaster you have ever witnessed was, at root, a length problem — the speaker mistaking the microphone for permission. Brevity is not modesty; it is power: the short toast lands whole, and leaves the room wanting more of you, which is the only condition to leave a room in.)

The hook — ten seconds that earn the room's ears: a specific image, a small story's opening, a gentle laugh. Not "for those who don't know me..." (they'll gather), not throat-clearing about being bad at speeches (never apologize in advance; it merely instructs the room what to notice).

The heart — the one true thing. Not three things: one — a single specific story or quality that makes the honoree visible ("I once watched Marcus drive four hours in the rain because a friend's voice sounded wrong on the phone — that's the whole man, and tonight you all get to know it"). Specificity is the entire difference between a toast and a greeting card; the room should learn something, or recognize something, about a person they thought they knew.

The lift — the turn to the room and the raising of the glass: the wish, aimed forward ("To Elena and Marcus — may the road rise to meet you, and may you always drive through the rain for each other"). Cue the room plainly — raise your glass and name the honoree — because a toast is a collective act and the speaker's last job is conducting it.

The etiquette in brief: the host toasts first (guests thereafter); stand if the room is standing-sized; the honoree does not drink to themselves — they receive, and may reply; look at the honoree during the heart and the room during the lift; and across much of Europe, meet each eye as you clink — the locals notice, and the old superstitions about failing to are colorful. What never enters a toast: the roast material (inside jokes exclude the room; embarrassing stories are a dominance display at the worst possible altar — if in doubt, run it past the four gates with the honoree's grandmother imagined present), the exes, the grievances, the speaker's own résumé, and the second drink's judgment — write it sober, deliver it on your first glass.

IIIThe 25 Toasts

A working armory — classics of the public domain and originals in this book's voice. Learn three by heart; adapt freely; the best toast is always the one only you could give, but a man should never stand empty-handed.

Classic & traditional

  1. May the road rise up to meet you, may the wind be always at your back, may the sun shine warm upon your face — and until we meet again, may God hold you in the palm of His hand. (Irish)
  2. May you live all the days of your life. (Swift)
  3. May the best of your past be the worst of your future. (Irish)
  4. May your home always be too small to hold all your friends. (Irish)
  5. May we be alive at this time next year. (Traditional)
  6. To absent friends. (Naval — two words, and often the heaviest toast of the night)
  7. May you get all your wishes but one, so you always have something to strive for. (Irish)
  8. A health to our sweethearts and wives — may they never meet. (Old Navy — deploy with care and a grin)
  9. May the hinges of our friendship never grow rusty. (Irish)
  10. To your good health, old friend — may you live for a thousand years, and I be there to count them. (after Robert Smith Surtees)

Weddings 11. To [names] — may your love be modern enough to survive the times, and old-fashioned enough to last forever. 12. Here's to the two of them: may every quarrel end in laughter, every silence be the comfortable kind, and every year make the wedding photos look like strangers who had no idea how good it would get. 13. May you grow old on one pillow. (Armenian) 14. To the bride and groom — may the road of your life be long, and may you always drive through the rain for each other. 15. Let anniversaries multiply like errors in a first draft — and may the story never need one.

Birthdays & milestones 16. To [name] — not older, but more. More stories, more friends, more reasons we're all glad you were born. 17. May your candles cost more than your cake. (Traditional) 18. To another year of getting away with it. 19. Here's to a man who makes growing older look like a promotion.

Farewells & new ventures 20. To the next chapter — may it be written by the same author and read by better critics. 21. May your new road be honest, your new friends be true, and your old ones be impossible to replace — which, glancing around this table, they are. 22. Fair winds and following seas. (Naval)

Gratitude & the table 23. To the cook — who has proven again that the shortest way to every heart in this room runs through this kitchen. 24. To our hosts: may what you gave tonight return to you tenfold — and may we be here to eat it. 25. To this table — the food was excellent, the wine was better, and the company made both look ordinary. May we never take it for granted, and never let a year pass without it.

IVThe Eulogy and the Room

Two further forms, in brief. The eulogy is the toast's solemn elder — same skeleton (hook, heart, lift), longer license (three to five minutes), and one governing permission that frees every frightened eulogist: your job is not to summarize a life; it is to bring the person into the room one more time. One or two true stories, told concretely, in your own faltering-is-fine voice, will do what no biography can — and laughter, arriving honestly inside grief, is not disrespect but the sound of the person actually arriving. Write it out fully; grief cannot improvise; and if your voice breaks, let it — a room at a funeral does not need your composure; it needs your truth, and the break is the tribute.

And the room at large — the meeting, the team, the hall — runs on everything already trained: one clear mission per address (Chapter 13's discipline, scaled), the confession of structure ("three things, then I'll stop" — audiences relax when they can see the shoreline), the deliberate pace and downward-settling voice of contained power, and the pause — the standing man's superweapon, which amateurs fear as dead air and masters deploy as emphasis. Twain's line above is the craft's whole open secret: the effortless speaker is the prepared one. Spontaneity, in public speech as in jazz, is what thorough preparation looks like from the audience.

VThe Practice

Practice

This week: arm yourself before the need. Choose the toast you are most likely to owe within the year — there is one; look at the calendar — and write it now, to architecture: hook, heart, lift, ninety seconds. Say it aloud, standing, three times — once to the mirror, twice to the empty room — until the first sentence is bulletproof (the first sentence is the whole battle; everything after it is downhill). Then memorize two classics from the armory as your standing reserves. The tapped glass, whenever it comes, will find a different man than it would have found this morning.
