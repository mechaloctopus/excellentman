## APPENDIX B — THE ETERNAL STUDENT'S READING LIST
### A Starting Shelf for the Royal Curriculum of Chapter 8

This is not a complete bibliography of everything this manual draws on — a full accounting would run longer than the book itself. It is a shorter, deliberately generous starting shelf: the works most directly behind each Book, chosen so that two classics and one contemporary voice sit beside each other wherever possible, per Chapter 8's own ratio. Read with a pencil. Reread the five that change you.

**Book I — The Mind**
- Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* — a working emperor's private notebook on composure, duty, and contained power, quoted throughout this manual because no later writer has said it better.
- Epictetus, *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* — the Stoic source of the instrument-calibration this book calls self-honesty.
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* — the origin of Chapter 9's whole argument that virtue is a habit, not a possession.

**Book II — The Word**
- Marshall Rosenberg, *Nonviolent Communication* — the modern framework behind this manual's architecture for difficult conversations.
- Dale Carnegie, *How to Win Friends and Influence People* — dated in places, correct about names and listening in a way nothing since has improved on.
- Benjamin Franklin, *The Autobiography* — the daily-virtue audit this manual borrows for Chapter 7, in its original form.

**Book III — The Body**
- Xenophon, *Memorabilia* — the source of Chapter 19's Socrates, and the oldest surviving argument that a philosopher owes his body attention too.
- Alan Flusser, *Dressing the Man* — the serious tailor's-eye treatment of fit that Chapter 22 compresses into a chapter.

**Book IV — The Company**
- Emily Post, *Etiquette* — read the original, not the reputation; it is far more about kindness than about forks.
- Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin, *The Physiology of Taste* — the founding document of hosting as a serious art, two centuries early and still correct.
- Priya Parker, *The Art of Gathering* — the modern case for gathering on purpose, cited directly in Chapter 25.

**Book V — The Companion**
- Erich Fromm, *The Art of Loving* — the source of this manual's central claim that love is a practiced capacity, not a weather event.
- John Gottman, *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work* — the research behind this book's insistence that contempt, not conflict, ends relationships.
- Christopher Wallis, *Tantra Illuminated* — the sober scholarly guide behind Chapter 35's rescue of a misused word.

**Book VI — The World**
- Mark Twain, *The Innocents Abroad* — travel writing that is still the funniest and most honest argument against prejudice ever assembled from a steamer ticket.
- Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, *Wind, Sand and Stars* — the source of Chapter 38's whole philosophy of travelling light, and Chapter 36's definition of partnership.

**Book VII — The Guardian**
- Gavin de Becker, *The Gift of Fear* — the essential text behind Chapter 41's whole argument that unexplained unease is data, not rudeness to override.
- Sun Tzu, *The Art of War* — read for the parts about winning without fighting, which are most of it and the least quoted part of it.

**Book VIII — The Service**
- Charles MacPherson, *The Butler's Guide* — a contemporary professional's account of the household discipline behind Chapter 44.
- Ian Fleming, the Bond novels themselves, not only the films — colder, sparer, and closer to Chapter 46's actual argument than their adaptations.

**Book IX — The Crown**
- Plato, *The Republic* — the source of the philosopher-king and the cave, in full, worth reading past the famous parts.
- Sir Thomas Malory, *Le Morte d'Arthur* — the Round Table and the Fisher King, in the language that has carried them for five centuries.
- The Bhagavad Gita and the principal Upanishads, in any respected translation — the deepest sources behind Chapter 50's closing argument, worth meeting directly rather than only through this book's summary of them.
